TRAINING STYLES

Training

Training is a crucial aspect of fitness and bodybuilding. It involves a structured program of physical exercises designed to improve strength, endurance, flexibility, and overall fitness. As mentioned in the Fitness page, physical activity for at least 30 minutes can be very beneficial to your health by raising blood flow, burning calories, reducing chances of getting diseases (such as type 2 diabetes, cancer, cardiovascular issues and others), etc. Different types of training are available to target different groups of people. An example being bodybuilder training for those who want big muscles, calisthenics for those who want to master body weight excersizes, specified training for different sports, etc. For instance, Training and running for longer periods of time increases your aerobic capacity (lung/O2 capacity) and Lactate Threshold (lactic acid hits you slower meaning slower sense of fatigue). This website has lists of many workouts for each muscle group for home workouts and gym workouts. Running more allows your body to adapt to the stress during training and your muscles start to adapt as well. You also build mental resilience over time. These are the key aspects of most training techniques:

  • Types of Training: There are various types of training, including strength training, cardiovascular training, flexibility training, and functional training.
  • Progressive Overload: To build muscle and strength, it's important to gradually increase the weight or resistance used in your workouts.
  • Rest and Recovery: Allowing your muscles to recover is essential for growth. Ensure you have rest days in your training program.
  • Nutrition: Proper nutrition supports your training goals. Fuel your body with the right nutrients before and after workouts.
  • Consistency: Consistency is key to achieving fitness goals. Stick to your training program and make it a regular part of your routine.
  • Hypertrophy: Hypertrophy is the terminology for muscle growth. It is achieved through Muscle Fiber Damage (stretch and tiny tears, not completely tearing), satellite cell activation, protein synthesis (rate increases post workouts) and through the influence of your hormones. Strength developed over time after progressive overloading (increasing the weights you lift overtime) and increased muscle fiber size. Resilience increases after adapting to your long term training which also means your body would recover from soreness faster.
    Hypertrophy is split into two parts:

    • Myofibrillar Hypertrophy: Myofibrillar hypertrophy is the increase in the size and density of myofibrils, which are the contractile units of muscle fibers. This type of hypertrophy is associated with strength gains and is typically achieved through heavy lifting and lower repetitions (1-6 reps).
    Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy: Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy is the increase in the volume of sarcoplasm, the semi-fluid substance surrounding myofibrils in muscle fibers. This type of hypertrophy is associated with increased endurance and is typically achieved through higher repetitions (8-15 reps) and moderate weights.

Types of Training

There are several types of training, each with its own focus and benefits. Here are some common types of training:

  • Bodybuilding Training: Bodybuilding training is designed to increase muscle size and definition. It typically involves a combination of strength training and nutrition strategies.
    General muscle growth (hypertrophy) is split into three parts:

    • Cutting: Cutting is the bodybuilding phase that involves losing a lot of body fat while also trying to preserve as much muscle as possible. This is achieved through a Calorie Deficit (eating around 250-500 less calories than your maintenance calories, which is your BMR). Your macrosâ€™ ratio during a cut should be lowered by a little (could be the same percentage as your maintenance calories). Once the desired Body Fat Percentage or weight is achieved you should start gradually increase back your maintenance calories to maintain. This is very effective in losing fat and weight, but not very effective in muscle growth. During cuts, you are to do around 30 minutes of cardio post workout to burn extra calories and fat. Compared to Body Recomp, this approach is a bit more extreme.
    Bulking: Bulking is the opposite of cutting. It focuses mostly on gaining muscle and strength while controlling the amount of fat you gain at the same time. This is achieved through a calorie surplus (eating 250-500 calories over your maintenance calories), which provides enough energy for your body to push those heavier weights while also growing your muscle. There are two ways to bulk; Lean bulk (eating whole foods and controlling the amount of junk food and excess fat you get) and Dirty bulking (you eat way over your maintenance calories with mostly junk food and a lot of excess nutrition). This is excellent for muscle and weight growth but bad for losing fat and weight.
    Once the desired weight is achieved you should start gradually decrease/increase back your maintenance calories to maintain. During bulking, you are to do around 30 minutes of cardio post workout to burn extra calories and fat. Compared to Cutting, this approach is a bit more extreme.
    Body Recomp: Body Recomposition (Recomping) refers to simultaneously gaining muscle while losing fat (weight) at the same time. This is a slower yet safer approach compared to the other two I will talk about. This requires meticulous attention to nutrition, training, and recovery. This approach focuses more on body composition more than solely on fat loss, weight loss or muscle gain. Body recomp is used to make your body first prepared for fat loss while gaining muscle at the same time. Compared to the other two, this is ok at gaining muscle, losing fat and losing weight.

  • Strength Training: This type of training focuses on building muscle strength and size through resistance exercises, such as weightlifting.
  • Cardiovascular Training: Cardiovascular training, or aerobic exercise, improves heart and lung health. Examples include running, cycling, and swimming.
  • Flexibility Training: Flexibility training enhances the range of motion in joints and muscles. Stretching exercises are commonly used for this purpose.
  • Functional Training: Functional training mimics everyday movements to improve overall functional fitness. It often includes bodyweight exercises and stability work.
  • High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT): HIIT involves short bursts of intense exercise followed by brief rest periods. It's an efficient way to improve cardiovascular fitness and burn calories.
  • Calisthenics: Calisthenics uses body weight for resistance training. It includes exercises like push-ups, pull-ups, and squats.
  • Sport-Specific Training: This type of training is tailored to improve performance in a specific sport, focusing on skills and movements relevant to that sport.
  • Endurance Training: Endurance training focuses on improving stamina and aerobic capacity. It often involves long-duration, moderate-intensity activities.
  • Power Training: Power training emphasizes explosive movements and speed. It is often used in sports that require quick bursts of energy.
  • Mobility Training: Mobility training focuses on improving joint range of motion and flexibility. It can help prevent injuries and enhance overall movement quality.
  • Core Training: Core training targets the muscles of the abdomen, lower back, and pelvis. A strong core is essential for stability and balance.
  • Balance Training: Balance training improves stability and coordination. It often includes exercises that challenge balance and proprioception.
  • Recovery Training: Recovery training focuses on gentle movements and stretching to promote recovery and reduce muscle soreness.
  • Powerlifting Training: Powerlifting training focuses on three main lifts: squat, bench press, and deadlift. It aims to increase maximal strength in these lifts.
  • Olympic Weightlifting: Olympic weightlifting involves two main lifts: the snatch and the clean and jerk. It requires explosive power and technique.
  • CrossFit: CrossFit is a high-intensity training program that combines elements of weightlifting, cardio, and functional movements.
  • Yoga: Yoga combines physical postures, breathing exercises, and meditation to improve flexibility, strength, and mental well-being.
  • Pilates: Pilates focuses on core strength, flexibility, and body awareness through controlled movements and exercises.
  • Group Fitness Classes: Group fitness classes offer a variety of workouts, such as Zumba, spinning, and boot camp, led by an instructor.
  • Outdoor Training: Outdoor training includes activities like hiking, trail running, and outdoor boot camps, taking advantage of natural environments.
  • Personal Training: Personal training involves working with a certified trainer who creates a customized workout plan based on individual goals and needs.
  • Online Training: Online training programs and apps provide access to workouts and coaching from the comfort of home.
  • Self-Defense Training: Self-defense training teaches techniques to protect oneself in dangerous situations. It often includes martial arts and combat training.
  • Rehabilitation Training: Rehabilitation training is designed to help individuals recover from injuries or surgeries. It focuses on restoring strength and mobility.
  • Sports Conditioning: Sports conditioning prepares athletes for the physical demands of their specific sport, enhancing performance and reducing injury risk.
  • Agility Training: Agility training improves quickness, coordination, and reaction time. It often includes ladder drills and cone exercises.
  • Speed Training: Speed training focuses on improving running speed and acceleration through specific drills and techniques.
  • Endurance Sports Training: Endurance sports training is tailored for athletes participating in long-distance events, such as marathons or triathlons.
  • Circuit Traininga form of body conditioning that involves endurance training, resistance training, high-intensity aerobics, and exercises done in a 'circular' repetition (circuits).
  • Tabata Training: Tabata training is a form of high-intensity interval training (HIIT) that consists of 20 seconds of intense exercise followed by 10 seconds of rest, repeated for a total of 4 minutes.
  • Bodyweight Training: Bodyweight training uses the individual's own weight as resistance. It includes exercises like push-ups, squats, and lunges.

These are just a few examples of the many types of training available. The choice of training type depends on individual goals, preferences, and fitness levels. It's important to choose a training program that aligns with your objectives and keeps you motivated. The most important aspect of training is your determination and mindset, whether you are willingly wanting to go on doing it again or not.
